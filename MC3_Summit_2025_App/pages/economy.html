Economic Trends & Analysis

Employment Status & Trends

Employment & Unemployment Trends (2015-2024)

Monroe County's labor market tells a story of resilience and transformation: Unemployment dropped from a pandemic peak of 12.4% in April 2020 to just 3.2% today, falling below pre-pandemic levels. More importantly, we've seen a fundamental shift in employment quality.

The data reveals strategic economic growth: Healthcare and education sectors now represent 62% of total employment, providing stable, family-sustaining jobs. Technology sector employment doubled since 2020, while manufacturing jobs decreased 15%, indicating our successful transition to a knowledge-based economy that directly benefits families with children.

Data Sources: Bureau of Labor Statistics, Indiana Department of Workforce Development

Income Distribution & Poverty

Household Income Distribution

Income inequality remains Monroe County's most pressing challenge: While median household income rose to $52,341, the gap between high and low earners widened. The top 20% of earners now make 4.2 times more than the bottom 20%, up from 3.8 times in 2015.

Children bear the heaviest burden: Our analysis shows 18.4% of children live in poverty, compared to 12.1% of adults. Single-mother households face the steepest challenges, with 34.7% living below the poverty line despite 68% employment rates, highlighting the critical need for affordable childcare and living-wage jobs.

Data Sources: S1901, S1902, S1903 Income Census Data

Housing Costs & Affordability

Housing Cost Burden Analysis

Housing affordability has reached a crisis point: 47.3% of Monroe County families now spend more than 30% of income on housing (the federal affordability threshold), up from 38.1% in 2015. For renters, the situation is more dire - 58% are cost-burdened, with median rent consuming 41% of median renter income.

The geography of opportunity is shifting: Affordable housing increasingly concentrates in areas with limited access to quality schools and services. Families paying less than $800/month in rent are 2.3 times more likely to live in neighborhoods with lower-performing schools, creating a cycle where housing affordability and educational opportunity become inversely related.

Data Sources: American Community Survey, Local Housing Authority
